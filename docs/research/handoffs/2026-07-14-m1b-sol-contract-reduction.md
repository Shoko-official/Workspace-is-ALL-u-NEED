## Decision in one sentence

The proposed map `T(Ω,G,B)`, implementation-neutral adequacy contracts, feasibility and efficiency boundaries, and abstention rule are exactly reconstructible from assume/guarantee contracts plus QoS/SLO predicates, temporal and provenance semantics, vector resource contracts, online caching, metareasoning, robust Pareto selection, and reject-option decision theory; the only possible residual would be a frozen, quantitatively falsifiable, LLM-specific ex-ante boundary law, and no such law or estimator is presently supplied.

## Scope and burden of proof

This audit tests the proposed directional formalism, not whether persistent state, retrieval, adaptive computation, provenance, or abstention can be useful in deployed LLM systems. The novelty burden is stronger than finding a useful vocabulary or combining known requirements in one document. At least one technical object must survive substitution by the strongest plausible composite of prior abstractions and must make a falsifiable prediction that the composite does not already make.

The audit therefore grants the candidate its strongest implementation-neutral interpretation. Named components are erased. A design is treated only as a causal behavior over observable traces, and resource budgets remain vectors. This avoids manufacturing novelty from architecture labels, scalar cost functions, or a contract that silently requires the component it purports to derive.

The result is negative. The formal shell is useful for evaluation design, but it does not establish a new theory, decision object, or boundary law.

## 1. Canonical candidate

### 1.1 Traces and implementations

Let `H` be the set of finite histories generated by an environment and a system. A trace records at least requests, accessible observations, outputs, state-change events, provenance and version metadata, resource measurements, and declared failures. Let

\[
\tau=(e_{0:n},o_{0:n},y_{0:n},q_{0:n},r_{0:n})\in\mathcal T,
\]

where `e` denotes environment events, `o` accessible observations, `y` externally visible outputs, `q` state or provenance events, and `r` a vector of measured resource use. An implementation `I` is any causal stochastic transducer from accessible histories to distributions over the next externally visible action and event. This definition encodes no topology.

### 1.2 Operational domain `Ω`

Use the tuple

\[
\Omega=(\mathcal E_\Omega,P_\Omega,\phi_\Omega,U_\Omega),
\]

where:

- `E_Ω` is the set of admissible environment histories;
- `P_Ω` is an optional sampling distribution or uncertainty set;
- `φ_Ω` is the feature map available before observing the outcome to be predicted;
- `U_Ω` freezes the population, request unit, time horizon, and sampling protocol.

The tuple makes ex-ante evaluation possible, but it does not prove that the chosen features are sufficient. In particular, labels such as dependency density, semantic compressibility, task volatility, reuse, or verification value are not predictive variables until they have an observable estimator, a frozen measurement time, and an error model.

### 1.3 Guarantee contract `G`

Let

\[
G=(A,\{(\Gamma_j,Q_j,\epsilon_j)\}_{j=1}^{m},O_G,F_G),
\]

where `A` is an assumption predicate on environment traces, `Γ_j` is an observable guarantee predicate, `Q_j` states its quantification mode, `ε_j` is its tolerance, `O_G` is the oracle or measurement procedure, and `F_G` is the declared response when the guarantee cannot be established. Quantification modes may be pathwise, worst-case, expectation, probability, or quantile. Examples include factual validity, revocation compliance, temporal consistency, provenance completeness, latency, availability, and bounded error.

This is an assume/guarantee contract enriched with standard QoS/SLO measurement semantics. It is not tied to memory, retrieval, recurrence, or any other component.

### 1.4 Resource envelope `B`

Let

\[
B=\{(R_k,b_k,h_k,S_k)\}_{k=1}^{d},
\]

where `R_k(τ)` is an observable resource measure, `b_k` its cap, `h_k` its accounting horizon, and `S_k` its enforcement semantics: hard, expected, probabilistic, or quantile. Axes can include FLOPs, accelerator time, wall-clock latency, HBM, transferred bytes, durable storage, reads and writes, network traffic, energy, and maintenance work.

Adequacy never scalarizes `B`. A scalar utility may be introduced only as a separately declared preference for comparing already adequate alternatives.

### 1.5 Behavioral design classes

The relevant classes can be defined without component names:

- `D_0(W)`: bounded transient or view-only behavior. If two histories expose the same current accessible view of size at most `W`, the implementation induces the same next-output distribution.
- `D_p`: behavior may depend on retained information that crosses request or episode boundaries.
- `D_f(N)`: a fixed, input-independent computation schedule bounded by `N` steps.
- `D_a(N_max)`: a bounded schedule may stop, route, or allocate additional computation as a function of accessible instance information, with maximum `N_max`.

These are observational classes. A prompt replay, database lookup, event-log fold, recurrent state, checkpoint, cache, and external service can instantiate the same class if their observable behavior is the same.

### 1.6 Adequacy and its evidence status

For a pathwise contract,

\[
I\models_\Omega(G,B)
\iff
\forall e\in\mathcal E_\Omega:\ A(e)\Rightarrow
\bigwedge_j\Gamma_j(\tau(I,e))
\land
\bigwedge_k R_k(\tau(I,e))\le b_k.
\]

The statistical form replaces the universal predicates by the quantifiers declared in `Q_j` and `S_k`, without converting distinct axes into one number. Finite evidence yields a three-valued certificate

\[
\operatorname{Cert}_D(\Omega,G,B)\in
\{\texttt{PASS},\texttt{FAIL},\texttt{UNRESOLVED}\},
\]

according to whether simultaneous confidence regions lie entirely inside, entirely outside, or straddle the admissible set. `UNRESOLVED` is not evidence that a class is adequate or inadequate.

### 1.7 Feasibility, efficiency, and `T`

For a behavioral class `D`, define

\[
F_D(\Omega,G,B)=
\mathbf 1\{\exists I\in D:I\models_\Omega(G,B)\}.
\]

Persistent cross-instance state is **required** only if `F_{D_0}=0` and `F_{D_p}=1`. The first conjunct needs an impossibility result or lower bound; the second needs a witness. This is a correct definition, not a predictive theory.

For adequate implementations only, let

\[
z(I)=(\ell_q,v_1,\ldots,v_m,c_1,\ldots,c_d),
\]

where `ℓ_q` is quality loss, `v_j` contract violation, and `c_k` resource use. Efficiency is the Pareto partial order on `z`. A **preferred** class requires either robust dominance or a preference functional fixed before results are seen. A claim of rank reversal is otherwise undefined: changing an unreported scalarization can reverse the rank by construction.

The strongest defensible selector is set-valued:

\[
T(\Omega,G,B)=
\begin{cases}
\operatorname{Pareto}\{D:F_D=1\}, & \text{if adequacy is resolved};\\
\texttt{ABSTAIN}, & \text{if confidence sets cross feasibility or ordering boundaries}.
\end{cases}
\]

Adaptive computation is selected when its conditional value of computation exceeds its compute and control overhead. This is the metareasoning rule

\[
\mathbb E[\Delta U\mid\phi_\Omega]>c_{\mathrm{compute}}+c_{\mathrm{control}}.
\]

Consequently, `T` is a generic robust constrained-selection rule unless the work supplies a calibrated predictor `\widehat T(φ_Ω,G,B)`, with thresholds and margins frozen before outcomes.

## 2. Exact generic boundary for view-only feasibility

Define visible-view equivalence by

\[
h\sim_Vh'\iff V(h)=V(h'),
\]

and let `Y_G(h)` be the set of outputs allowed by `G` after history `h`.

**Proposition.** A deterministic view-only implementation satisfying `G` exists if and only if

\[
\forall C\in H/{\sim_V}:\quad \bigcap_{h\in C}Y_G(h)\ne\varnothing.
\]

**Necessity.** A view-only implementation must emit the same output for every history in one equivalence class. That output must be acceptable for each member, so it belongs to the intersection.

**Sufficiency.** Choose one member of the non-empty intersection for every equivalence class and emit it for the corresponding visible view.

For stochastic outputs, replace each acceptable-output set by linear constraints on one distribution shared by the equivalence class. Feasibility is then an ordinary linear feasibility problem.

This proposition gives the cleanest implementation-neutral account of when a bounded accessible view is insufficient. It is also a generic observational-equivalence and realizability result. It uses no property specific to language models, tokens, autoregression, or natural language. Calling the equivalence classes “contexts” does not create a new boundary theorem.

## 4. Break-even models and countermodels

### 4.1 Generic break-even equations

For reuse count `R`, avoided per-use work `s`, maintenance cost `m`, error probability `p_e`, and error loss `L`, the net value of retained state is

\[
g(R)=Rs-m-p_eL.
\]

State wins only above the ordinary reuse threshold `R>(m+p_eL)/s`. At `R=0`, it cannot be justified by reuse. This is a cache/materialized-view calculation, not an LLM-specific boundary.

For adaptive computation with observable instance features `φ`, it is worth invoking only when

\[
\mathbb E[\Delta U\mid\phi]>c+o,
\]

where `c` is added compute and `o` is router, calibration, and scheduling overhead. On a homogeneous easy workload with negligible improvement, a fixed schedule wins. This is value of computation.

If uncertainty intervals for either expression cross zero, the identified action is `ABSTAIN`, collect more evidence, or return several nondominated classes. That is standard decision-making under partial identification.

### 4.2 Orthogonal-instantiation countermodel

Take one contract requiring that every answer cite the version of each fact, reject facts invalidated before request time, and reproduce the state visible at an earlier transaction time. It can be satisfied by at least three non-isomorphic implementations:

1. replay the complete signed event history inside the current accessible input;
2. query a bitemporal database and return a provenance bundle;
3. use checkpointed retained state plus an invalidation log.

The same contract admits all three. Therefore temporal, provenance, and revocation guarantees do not imply one architecture family. If the contract instead requires a named persistent component, it has encoded its conclusion and fails implementation neutrality.

### 4.3 Negative regimes

- **No-reuse regime.** Independent one-shot requests arrive once, all relevant immutable evidence fits the accessible view, and durable reads/writes add latency. View-only behavior can dominate retained state.
- **High-volatility regime.** Retained facts become stale faster than invalidation propagates. A favored persistent topology violates the revocation contract even though it has more memory.
- **Full-history-visible regime.** Complete relevant event history fits the current input. Replay can satisfy temporal and provenance requirements without cross-request hidden state.
- **Uniform-easy regime.** Every request is solved by the minimum schedule. Adaptive routing adds overhead without quality gain, so fixed computation dominates.
- **Low-signal regime.** Ex-ante covariates do not identify which class will be adequate. Any forced directional recommendation exceeds the evidence; abstention is correct.
- **Budget-axis conflict.** One class uses fewer FLOPs but more durable I/O and tail latency. Neither dominates. Any unique rank is an unstated preference, not a boundary discovery.

These regimes satisfy the requested anti-universality discipline, but they are generic countermodels. No evidence supplied so far turns them into validated LLM regime laws.

## 7. What would be required to reopen the route

The route can survive only if later work supplies all of the following:

1. At least one pre-outcome, operationally measurable LLM-specific variable or invariant that cannot be reduced to reuse, locality, information sufficiency, generic uncertainty, or value of computation.
2. A frozen predictor, threshold, and non-zero decision margin for `\widehat T(φ_Ω,G,B)` before evaluating outcomes.
3. Either a lower bound/impossibility theorem or a replicated quantitative rank reversal under matched vector budgets and complete state-maintenance accounting.
4. Coverage of at least two non-isomorphic implementation families, so the result is not a component benchmark in contract notation.
5. A component-neutral admission rule and observable oracle for every guarantee.
6. Explicit negative regimes and a calibrated abstention region with stated error or coverage guarantees.
7. Robust dominance, or a preference/scalarization declared before results, for every claim that one adequate class is “better.”

Until those conditions are met, the contract formalism may be used as benchmark hygiene, but it must not be presented as the article's original contribution or as a directional law for future LLM design.

## 10. Final disposition

Verdict: `STOP` for the contract/formalism route.
